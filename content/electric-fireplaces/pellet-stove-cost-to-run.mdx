# How Much Does a Pellet Stove Cost to Run? (Per Month)

A pellet stove costs **$150 to $350 per month** to run during the heating season, including pellet fuel ($120 to $300/month) and electricity for the auger and fans ($10 to $15/month). For a typical 1,800 sq ft home in a cold climate (zone 5), expect roughly **$250/month** in total operating costs, or about $1,250 to $1,500 for a full October-through-March heating season.

That makes pellet stoves among the most cost-effective heating options available in 2026. Wood pellets cost $14 to $19 per million BTU of useful heat, which undercuts electricity ($33 to $55/MMBTU) and competes favorably with natural gas ($10 to $25/MMBTU) in most markets. Below, we break down every cost component so you can calculate your exact numbers.

## Pellet Fuel: The Biggest Cost

Pellet fuel accounts for 85 to 90 percent of your total operating cost. Here are the fundamental numbers for 2026:

**Pellet pricing basics:**
- 1 ton = 50 bags at 40 lbs each = 2,000 lbs total
- National average price: $280/ton for premium grade
- Per-bag price: $5.00 to $6.20 for premium
- Energy content: 8,000 to 8,500 BTU per pound (premium grade)
- Gross energy per ton: 16 to 17 million BTU
- Useful energy per ton at 80% efficiency: 12.8 to 13.6 million BTU

## Money-Saving Strategies

**1. Buy pellets in bulk during spring/summer.** A 3-ton order in June typically costs 10 to 15 percent less than buying bags individually during winter. That is $84 to $126 in savings on a 3-ton purchase.

**2. Use the thermostat.** Letting the stove cycle between high and low based on room temperature uses 20 to 30 percent less fuel than running it continuously on medium. The stove idles on low when the room reaches target temp, then ramps up when it drops.

**3. Seal and insulate.** Every air leak and insulation gap means your stove works harder. Weather-stripping windows ($50 to $100 DIY) and adding attic insulation ($500 to $1,500) can reduce heating demand by 15 to 30 percent, directly cutting pellet consumption.

**4. Use ceiling fans.** A ceiling fan on low speed (clockwise in winter) pushes warm air pooling at the ceiling back down to the living space. This simple trick can reduce the stove's run time by 10 to 15 percent in rooms with 8-foot-plus ceilings.

**5. Zone heat strategically.** If your pellet stove heats the main living area, close doors to unused rooms and let those spaces stay cooler. A 2,200 sq ft home effectively becomes a 1,200 sq ft heating zone, cutting pellet usage nearly in half.

**6. Maintain your stove religiously.** A dirty heat exchanger can reduce efficiency by 10 to 20 percent. Weekly burn-pot cleaning and monthly firebox vacuuming keep the stove operating at peak efficiency throughout the season.

**7. Claim the federal tax credit.** High-efficiency pellet stoves (75 percent or above, HHV) qualify for a 26 percent federal tax credit under the IRA's Section 25D. On a $4,500 installed stove, that is $1,170 back on your taxes. This does not reduce operating costs, but it significantly reduces your effective purchase price and shortens the payback period.

## Power Outage Considerations

Since pellet stoves require electricity, you need a plan for power outages. Options include:

**Battery backup systems ($200 to $500):** Dedicated pellet stove battery backups can run the stove for 8 to 24 hours depending on battery capacity and stove wattage. The Minuteman or CyberPower UPS systems designed for 300W-plus loads work well. Make sure the battery's watt-hour rating exceeds your stove's draw multiplied by desired run hours.

**Portable generator ($400 to $1,200):** A 2,000W inverter generator provides clean power for the stove plus basic household needs. Pellet stoves typically draw 150 to 300W during operation, so even a small generator has headroom. Always operate generators outdoors and away from windows.

**Gravity-fed wood stove as backup:** Some households maintain a small wood stove or space heater as a backup that requires no electricity. This belt-and-suspenders approach ensures you are never without heat.
